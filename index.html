What If the Buddha Had Been a Woman?

What if the Buddha had been a woman?
She leaves her sleeping child to seek the truth.
And the world calls her selfish — not enlightened.

It’s a question that cuts deep. Not into doctrine, but into the collective bias we’ve inherited. In a world that venerates sacrifice when made by men, but calls it abandonment when done by women, what room is left for female awakening?

His Holiness the Dalai Lama has long taught that enlightenment is not limited by gender. He has said again and again:

“We all have the same potential. The Buddha gave equal opportunity to men and women.”
He even suggests that his own reincarnation could be female — if the world needs it.

In Buddhism, what matters is intention — the clarity of the heart, not the shape of the body.

If a woman walked away from all that tethered her — not out of ego, but out of a deep wish to awaken for the benefit of all beings — is that not compassion in its highest form?

This isn’t hypothetical. It’s historical.

  • Tārā, the beloved female bodhisattva of compassion, vowed to always be reborn as a woman to challenge the notion that only men could become enlightened.
  • Prajñāpāramitā, the “Mother of All Buddhas,” is the very embodiment of transcendent wisdom.
  • Yeshe Tsogyal, the great Tibetan yogini, reached full awakening and is revered to this day.
  • Maha Pajapati Gotami, the Buddha’s own aunt, became the first ordained nun and helped establish the bhikkhunī sangha for generations of women seekers.

These women did not wait for permission to be free.
They stepped onto the path — and walked it with courage, clarity, and compassion.

So when we hear:
“A woman left her child to find herself.”
And the world says:
“How selfish.”
We might pause… and remember that the world has always misjudged women who wake up.

And maybe, just maybe — she wasn’t running away. She was walking toward truth.

Written with inspiration from the teachings of His Holiness the 14th Dalai Lama.
